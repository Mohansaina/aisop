## 1. Purpose

This Maintenance Operations Standard Operating Procedure (SOP) establishes the processes governing repair requests, preventive maintenance, life-safety system upkeep, and general facilities upkeep across guest rooms, back-of-house areas, and public spaces. It ensures that maintenance issues (also referred to as "repair requests," "engineering tickets," or "work orders") are logged, prioritized, resolved, and documented consistently, minimizing guest impact and protecting the property's physical assets and safety systems.

This document also defines preventive maintenance scheduling, urgent versus routine classification, and coordination with Front Desk and Housekeeping, and serves as an authoritative reference within the AI Operations Assistant knowledge base for maintenance-related employee questions.

---

## 2. Scope

This SOP applies to all Engineering and Maintenance personnel, including:

- Maintenance Technicians (also referred to as "Engineers" or "Facilities Technicians")
- Chief Engineer / Director of Engineering
- Maintenance Supervisors
- Contracted/outsourced maintenance vendors working under Engineering oversight

It covers guest room repair requests, public area and back-of-house facilities maintenance, preventive maintenance scheduling, life-safety system inspection and testing, and Out of Order (OOO)/Out of Service (OOS) room status coordination. It does not cover Housekeeping cleaning procedures, Food Safety equipment sanitation standards, or IT/network infrastructure support, which are addressed in separate departmental SOPs.

---

## 3. Definitions

- **Work Order (also called "Repair Request," "Maintenance Ticket," or "Engineering Ticket")**: A documented request for maintenance action, including the reported issue, location, priority level, and assigned technician.
- **Preventive Maintenance (PM)**: Scheduled, proactive maintenance performed on a regular interval to prevent equipment failure, as opposed to reactive repair after a failure occurs.
- **OOO (Out of Order)**: A room or area status indicating it is not sellable or usable due to a maintenance issue requiring repair before it can return to service.
- **OOS (Out of Service)**: A room or area status indicating a temporary, typically shorter-term restriction than OOO, often used for minor issues not requiring extended repair time.
- **Urgent Work Order**: A maintenance issue posing immediate safety risk, guest impact, or operational disruption, requiring response within a compressed timeframe.
- **Routine Work Order**: A non-urgent maintenance issue that can be scheduled within standard response timeframes without immediate guest or safety impact.
- **Life-Safety System**: Critical building systems including fire alarms, sprinklers, fire extinguishers, emergency lighting, and backup generators, subject to enhanced inspection and testing requirements.
- **Vendor / Contractor**: An external service provider engaged for specialized repairs or maintenance beyond in-house Engineering capability.
- **Asset Register**: The property's inventory of equipment and systems, including installation date, maintenance history, and expected service life, used to guide preventive maintenance scheduling.

## 4. Roles & Responsibilities

### Maintenance Technician
- Responds to assigned work orders per priority classification and completes repairs to brand quality standards.
- Documents work performed, parts used, and completion status on each work order.
- Reports any additional issues discovered during a repair (e.g., related equipment concerns) to the Supervisor.
- Performs assigned preventive maintenance tasks per the PM schedule.

### Maintenance Supervisor
- Assigns and prioritizes incoming work orders among available technicians.
- Monitors work order completion times against operational standards.
- Coordinates with Front Desk and Housekeeping on OOO/OOS room status changes.
- Escalates unresolved or recurring issues to the Chief Engineer/Director of Engineering.

### Chief Engineer / Director of Engineering
- Oversees the preventive maintenance program and Asset Register management.
- Ensures life-safety system inspections and testing are completed and documented per regulatory requirements.
- Manages vendor/contractor relationships for specialized repairs.
- Reviews maintenance budget, parts inventory, and capital repair planning.

### Front Desk (Liaison Responsibilities)
- Submits guest-reported maintenance issues promptly and accurately to Engineering.
- Coordinates room status updates (OOO/OOS) with Engineering to reflect current sellability.
- Communicates realistic repair timelines to affected guests based on Engineering input.

### Housekeeping (Liaison Responsibilities)
- Reports maintenance issues discovered during room servicing immediately.
- Coordinates room access scheduling with Engineering for non-urgent repairs during stayover periods.

## 5. Standard Operating Procedure

### Phase 1: Work Order Intake and Classification

**Purpose:** To ensure every reported maintenance issue is accurately logged and appropriately prioritized.

**Required Materials:** Work order system (digital or paper-based), priority classification guide.

**Step-by-Step Process:**
1. Receive the maintenance issue report from Front Desk, Housekeeping, a guest, or direct observation by any employee.
2. Log the work order with location, issue description, reporting source, and time received.
3. Classify the work order as Urgent or Routine based on the priority classification guide (e.g., safety risk, active leak, no heat/AC = Urgent; cosmetic or non-critical function issue = Routine).
4. Assign the work order to an available technician based on classification and current workload.
5. Notify Front Desk if the issue affects room sellability, recommending OOO/OOS status as appropriate.

**Quality Checkpoints:** Work orders logged within 5 minutes of receipt; priority classification applied consistently per the classification guide.

**Expected Outcome:** Every maintenance issue is accurately documented and appropriately prioritized for response.

**Escalation Conditions:** Any issue involving suspected gas leak, electrical hazard, or structural concern is escalated immediately to the Maintenance Supervisor and, if applicable, the Emergency Response Manual (ER-SOP-201) is activated.

---

### Phase 2: Urgent Work Order Response

**Purpose:** To resolve safety-critical or high-impact maintenance issues with minimal delay.

**Required Materials:** Technician tools and parts inventory, work order system, radio communication.

**Step-by-Step Process:**
1. Assigned technician responds to the location immediately upon notification.
2. Assess the issue and determine whether it can be resolved on-site or requires additional parts, vendor support, or room status change.
3. If the room or area cannot remain in service, notify the Supervisor to coordinate OOO/OOS status with Front Desk.
4. Complete the repair to brand quality standard, testing the resolved function before closing the work order.
5. Document the resolution, parts used, and time completed on the work order.
6. Notify Front Desk immediately upon completion if room status can be restored to sellable.

**Quality Checkpoints:** Urgent work orders responded to within 15 minutes; resolved or escalated to vendor support within 2 hours where feasible.

**Expected Outcome:** Safety-critical and high-impact issues are resolved quickly, minimizing guest disruption and safety risk.

**Escalation Conditions:** If the issue cannot be resolved in-house, escalate immediately to the Chief Engineer for vendor/contractor engagement.

---

### Phase 3: Routine Work Order Response

**Purpose:** To resolve non-urgent maintenance issues within standard timeframes without disrupting guest service.

**Required Materials:** Technician tools and parts inventory, work order system.

**Step-by-Step Process:**
1. Technician reviews the routine work order queue and schedules the repair based on priority order and room/guest availability.
2. Coordinate with Housekeeping or Front Desk for access scheduling if the area is occupied or requires guest notification.
3. Complete the repair to brand quality standard, testing the resolved function before closing the work order.
4. Document the resolution, parts used, and completion time on the work order.
5. Close the work order in the system and update any associated room status.

**Quality Checkpoints:** Routine work orders completed within 24 hours of receipt under standard conditions.

**Expected Outcome:** Non-urgent issues are resolved reliably without requiring guest disruption or urgent escalation.

**Escalation Conditions:** Any routine work order remaining open beyond 48 hours is escalated to the Maintenance Supervisor for review.

---

### Phase 4: Preventive Maintenance

**Purpose:** To proactively maintain equipment and systems, reducing the likelihood of unexpected failures and extending asset service life.

**Required Materials:** Preventive Maintenance schedule, Asset Register, technician tools.

**Step-by-Step Process:**
1. Chief Engineer maintains the PM schedule based on manufacturer recommendations and the Asset Register.
2. Technicians complete assigned PM tasks (e.g., HVAC filter changes, elevator inspection, pool equipment servicing) per the scheduled interval.
3. Document completion of each PM task, including any deficiencies identified during the process.
4. Any deficiency identified during PM is logged as a new work order for follow-up repair.
5. Update the Asset Register with maintenance history and any component replacements.

**Quality Checkpoints:** 100% of scheduled PM tasks completed within the defined interval window.

**Expected Outcome:** Equipment and systems remain in reliable working condition, minimizing reactive repair frequency.

**Escalation Conditions:** Any missed PM interval beyond the allowable grace period is escalated to the Director of Engineering for schedule review.

---

### Phase 5: Life-Safety System Inspection and Testing

**Purpose:** To ensure fire, life-safety, and emergency systems remain fully functional and compliant with regulatory requirements.

**Required Materials:** Life-safety inspection checklist, testing equipment, compliance documentation records.

**Step-by-Step Process:**
1. Conduct scheduled inspections and testing of fire alarms, sprinkler systems, fire extinguishers, emergency lighting, and backup generators per regulatory-required frequency.
2. Document each inspection/test result, including pass/fail status and any corrective action taken.
3. Any failed component is repaired or replaced immediately, with the system re-tested before being marked compliant.
4. Maintain all life-safety inspection records for the regulatory-required retention period, available for audit or inspection by authorities.
5. Notify the General Manager and Director of Security & Loss Prevention of any significant life-safety system deficiency.

**Quality Checkpoints:** 100% of life-safety inspections completed on schedule and documented; zero unresolved critical deficiencies.

**Expected Outcome:** All life-safety systems remain fully functional and compliant, supporting guest and employee safety.

**Escalation Conditions:** Any life-safety system failure that cannot be immediately corrected is escalated to the General Manager, and affected areas may require temporary closure per the Emergency Response Manual.

## 7. Best Practices

- **Classify urgency accurately and consistently, not based on who is asking.** Safety and guest-impact criteria should drive priority classification, not the persistence or seniority of the requester.
- **Document every work order thoroughly, even quick fixes.** A pattern of "quick fixes" without documentation often masks a larger recurring issue that preventive maintenance could otherwise catch.
- **Communicate realistic timelines to Front Desk and guests.** Overpromising repair speed erodes guest trust more than an honest, well-communicated delay.
- **Treat preventive maintenance as equally important as reactive repair.** PM compliance directly reduces urgent work order volume and unplanned OOO room inventory over time.
- **Never bypass life-safety testing protocols to save time.** These systems exist specifically for worst-case scenarios; consistent testing is the only way to ensure they function when needed.

## 8. Exceptions

**Guest Room Occupied During Urgent Repair:** Technicians coordinate with Front Desk to notify the guest before entry whenever possible, except in cases of immediate safety hazard requiring urgent access.

**VIP Guest Rooms:** No differentiated repair priority based on guest status alone; however, any issue affecting a VIP guest's room is communicated promptly to Guest Relations for coordinated guest communication.

**Vendor/Contractor-Dependent Repairs:** Specialized repairs beyond in-house capability (e.g., elevator major component failure) are escalated to the Chief Engineer for vendor engagement, with realistic timeline communication to Front Desk and affected guests.

**Life-Safety System Failures:** Any life-safety system failure takes precedence over all other work orders and may require temporary area closure or evacuation per the Emergency Response Manual.

**After-Hours/Overnight Emergencies:** On-call Engineering staff respond per the property's after-hours emergency contact protocol; Duty Manager coordinates guest communication during overnight urgent repairs.

## 9. Troubleshooting

**Problem: A guest reports the same issue has recurred after a previous repair.**
- *Possible Causes:* Incomplete original repair, underlying root cause not addressed, new unrelated failure.
- *Resolution:* Review the prior work order documentation, assess whether the root cause was addressed, and complete a thorough repair rather than a temporary fix.
- *Escalation:* Recurring issues on the same asset are escalated to the Maintenance Supervisor for root-cause review.

**Problem: A required part is not in inventory for an urgent repair.**
- *Possible Causes:* Inventory shortage, unusual/specialized part requirement.
- *Resolution:* Source the part through expedited ordering or an approved local vendor; provide a temporary mitigation if guest impact is significant (e.g., alternate room).
- *Escalation:* Notify the Maintenance Supervisor and Front Desk of the extended timeline for guest communication purposes.

**Problem: A preventive maintenance task is missed due to scheduling conflict.**
- *Possible Causes:* Staffing shortage, competing urgent work order volume.
- *Resolution:* Reschedule the PM task as soon as possible within the allowable grace period, documenting the delay reason.
- *Escalation:* Repeated PM scheduling gaps are escalated to the Director of Engineering for staffing or process review.

**Problem: A life-safety inspection reveals a failed component.**
- *Possible Causes:* Equipment age, wear, environmental factors.
- *Resolution:* Repair or replace the component immediately and re-test before marking the system compliant.
- *Escalation:* Any inspection failure is reported immediately to the Director of Engineering and General Manager.

## 10. Frequently Asked Questions

**1. How do I submit a maintenance request for a guest room?**
Submit a work order through the Engineering system with the room number, issue description, and priority indication; Front Desk or Housekeeping typically initiates guest-reported requests.

**2. What makes a work order "urgent" versus "routine"?**
Urgent work orders involve safety risk, active leaks, loss of heating/cooling, or significant guest impact; routine work orders are non-critical issues that can be scheduled within standard timeframes.

**3. How quickly should an urgent maintenance issue be addressed?**
Technicians respond within 15 minutes of notification, with resolution or vendor escalation targeted within 2 hours where feasible.

**4. What do I do if I discover a maintenance issue while cleaning a room?**
Report it immediately to your Supervisor and submit or request a work order be logged with Engineering.

**5. Can a room stay occupied while under repair?**
Only for non-invasive, low-disruption repairs; the guest should be notified in advance except in cases of immediate safety hazard requiring urgent access.

**6. What is Out of Order (OOO) versus Out of Service (OOS)?**
OOO indicates a room is not sellable due to a maintenance issue requiring repair, while OOS indicates a typically shorter-term restriction, often for a more minor issue.

**7. How often are life-safety systems inspected?**
Per regulatory-required frequency specific to each system type (fire alarms, sprinklers, extinguishers, emergency lighting, generators); documentation is maintained for the required retention period.

**8. What happens if a required repair part isn't in stock?**
The part is sourced through expedited ordering or an approved vendor, and a temporary mitigation (e.g., alternate room) may be offered if guest impact is significant.

**9. Who approves engaging an outside vendor for a repair?**
The Chief Engineer or Director of Engineering approves vendor/contractor engagement for specialized repairs beyond in-house capability.

**10. What is preventive maintenance, and why does it matter?**
Preventive maintenance is scheduled, proactive servicing performed to prevent equipment failure before it occurs, reducing reactive repair volume and extending asset service life.

**11. How do I know if a repair should be classified as safety-related?**
Refer to the priority classification guide; any issue posing immediate risk to guest or employee safety (e.g., gas smell, exposed wiring, structural concern) is classified as Urgent and escalated immediately.

**12. What should I do if I smell gas in a guest room or public area?**
Evacuate the immediate area, notify Engineering and Security immediately, and follow Emergency Response Manual procedures for suspected gas leaks.

**13. Do VIP guests get priority for maintenance repairs?**
No differentiated priority based on guest status alone; however, Guest Relations is notified promptly to coordinate communication for any VIP guest affected by a maintenance issue.

**14. How long can a routine work order remain open before it needs follow-up?**
Routine work orders should be completed within 24 hours; anything unresolved beyond 48 hours is escalated to the Maintenance Supervisor.

**15. What documentation is required after completing a repair?**
The technician documents the resolution, parts used, and completion time directly on the work order before closing it.

**16. Who handles maintenance issues after hours or overnight?**
On-call Engineering staff respond per the property's after-hours emergency contact protocol, coordinated with the Duty Manager.

**17. What is the Asset Register used for?**
It tracks equipment inventory, installation dates, maintenance history, and expected service life, guiding the preventive maintenance schedule.

**18. Can Housekeeping request a delay on a repair if a guest is sleeping or resting?**
Yes, for non-urgent repairs; Housekeeping and Engineering coordinate access scheduling around guest presence whenever the issue is not safety-critical.

**19. What happens if a life-safety inspection finds a failed fire alarm component?**
The component is repaired or replaced immediately and re-tested before the system is marked compliant; the failure is reported to the Director of Engineering and General Manager.

**20. How do I report a recurring issue that keeps coming back after repair?**
Note that the issue is recurring when submitting the work order, and it will be escalated for root-cause review rather than another temporary fix.

## 13. Training Scenarios

### Scenario 1: Guest Reports No Hot Water in Their Room
**Situation:** A guest calls the Front Desk reporting no hot water in their room during their evening routine.

**Correct Response:** The issue is logged as Urgent, a technician responds within 15 minutes, diagnoses the issue, and either resolves it promptly or arranges a room change while continuing to work the repair, with the guest kept informed throughout.

**Incorrect Response:** Logging the issue as Routine and leaving it in the standard queue without urgency, causing the guest extended inconvenience.

**Supervisor Notes:** Loss of hot water significantly affects guest comfort and should be classified and responded to as urgent, not treated as a minor inconvenience.

**Learning Outcome:** Accurate urgency classification directly protects the guest experience and should reflect real guest impact, not just technical severity.

---

### Scenario 2: Recurring Air Conditioning Failure in the Same Room
**Situation:** A technician is dispatched to a room for an air conditioning issue that was reportedly repaired just two weeks earlier.

**Correct Response:** The technician reviews the prior work order, investigates for an underlying root cause rather than repeating the same temporary fix, and documents findings for the Supervisor to review the asset's maintenance history.

**Incorrect Response:** Performing the identical quick fix without investigating why the issue recurred so soon.

**Supervisor Notes:** Recurring issues on the same asset within a short window are a signal to investigate root cause, not simply repeat the prior repair.

**Learning Outcome:** Thorough root-cause analysis prevents repeat guest impact and reduces long-term maintenance costs.

---

### Scenario 3: Fire Extinguisher Inspection Reveals an Expired Unit
**Situation:** During a routine life-safety inspection, a technician discovers a fire extinguisher in a public corridor has expired and failed pressure verification.

**Correct Response:** The technician immediately replaces the unit with a compliant, fully charged extinguisher, documents the finding and corrective action, and reports the deficiency per standard life-safety reporting procedure.

**Incorrect Response:** Noting the issue for a "future" replacement without immediate correction, leaving a non-compliant unit in place.

**Supervisor Notes:** Life-safety deficiencies require immediate correction, not deferred scheduling, given their direct safety implications.

**Learning Outcome:** Life-safety compliance requires zero-tolerance immediate correction of any identified deficiency.
