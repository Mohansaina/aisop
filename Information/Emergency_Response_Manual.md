## 1. Purpose

This Emergency Response Manual establishes the property-wide procedures for identifying, responding to, and recovering from emergency situations, including fire, medical emergencies, severe weather, security threats, power/system failures, and other life-safety incidents. It ensures every employee, regardless of department, understands their role during an emergency and can act quickly, calmly, and correctly to protect guests, employees, and the property.

This manual supersedes department-specific procedures during an active emergency; all staff defer to this document and the direction of the Duty Manager and Emergency Response Team until the situation is declared resolved.

---

## 2. Scope

This manual applies to all employees at every level and department, including Front Desk, Housekeeping, Food & Beverage, Engineering, Security, Administration, and all outsourced or contracted staff working on property. It covers:

- Fire emergencies and evacuation
- Medical emergencies
- Severe weather events
- Security threats (including suspicious persons, threats, and civil disturbances)
- Power outages and critical system failures
- Bomb threats
- Active threat situations

It does not cover routine guest complaint handling, standard maintenance requests, or non-emergency security matters (e.g., lost key reissuance), which are addressed in their respective departmental SOPs.

## 3. Definitions

- **Emergency Response Team (ERT)**: The designated group of trained employees, led by the Duty Manager or General Manager, responsible for coordinating the property's response during an emergency.
- **Duty Manager**: The manager on duty holding property-wide authority during a shift, including the authority to declare and manage an emergency response.
- **Evacuation**: The organized movement of guests and employees away from a hazard to a designated safe location, either horizontally (to another wing/floor) or vertically (out of the building) as conditions require.
- **Assembly Point (also called "Muster Point" or "Rally Point")**: A pre-designated outdoor or off-site location where evacuated guests and employees gather for headcount and further instruction.
- **Shelter-in-Place**: A protective action directing guests and employees to remain indoors, typically used during severe weather or certain security threats, rather than evacuating.
- **Code Alert System**: The property's internal communication protocol using coded phrases over radio or phone to notify staff of an emergency without alarming guests (e.g., "Code Red" for fire).
- **All Clear**: The official declaration, issued only by the Duty Manager, General Manager, or emergency services, that the emergency has been resolved and normal operations may resume.
- **First Responder**: External emergency services personnel, including fire department, police, or emergency medical services (EMS).
- **AED (Automated External Defibrillator)**: A portable device used to treat sudden cardiac arrest, located at designated points throughout the property.
- **Incident Commander**: The senior-most trained individual on-site directing the emergency response until first responders arrive and assume control.

## 4. Roles & Responsibilities

### All Employees
- Know the location of the nearest fire exits, fire extinguishers, and AEDs relative to their work area.
- Report any emergency immediately to the Front Desk, Security, or via the property's internal emergency line.
- Follow all instructions issued by the Emergency Response Team without delay.
- Assist guests calmly without creating panic.
- Never attempt to fight a fire beyond an incipient stage or take personal risks beyond their trained capability.

### Front Desk Agent
- Serves as the primary point of contact for emergency notification and coordinates communication with first responders upon arrival.
- Activates the Code Alert System upon confirmation of an emergency.
- Maintains a current in-house guest count for evacuation accountability.

### Duty Manager
- Assumes the role of Incident Commander until relieved by the General Manager or first responders.
- Declares the emergency response level and directs evacuation, shelter-in-place, or other protective action.
- Communicates with first responders upon arrival and provides property information (floor plans, guest counts, hazard locations).
- Issues the "All Clear" declaration once the situation is resolved.

### Security / Loss Prevention
- Responds immediately to the incident location.
- Assists with evacuation flow, crowd control, and access restriction as needed.
- Documents the incident timeline and actions taken.
- Coordinates directly with first responders on arrival.

### Engineering / Maintenance
- Shuts down relevant building systems (HVAC, elevators, gas lines) as directed during fire or gas-related emergencies.
- Assists with emergency lighting, generator activation, and life-safety system operation.
- Reports system status to the Duty Manager throughout the incident.

### Housekeeping
- Assists with guest floor sweeps during evacuation when directed by the Duty Manager or Security.
- Reports any guests requiring assistance encountered during floor sweeps.

### Food & Beverage
- Directs guests in restaurants, bars, and event spaces to evacuate or shelter per instructions.
- Assists with headcount coordination for guests in F&B outlets during the incident.

### Guest Relations
- Supports affected guests post-incident with communication, relocation, or compensation as directed by the Duty Manager or General Manager.

## 5. Standard Operating Procedure

### Phase 1: Emergency Detection and Notification

**Purpose:** To ensure any emergency is identified and communicated to the appropriate response personnel within seconds of detection.

**Required Materials:** Property radio system, Front Desk phone line, fire alarm panel, Code Alert reference card.

**Step-by-Step Process:**
1. The employee who detects or is informed of an emergency immediately contacts the Front Desk or Security via radio or internal emergency line.
2. Front Desk confirms the nature and location of the emergency.
3. Front Desk activates the Code Alert System, notifying all department heads and the Duty Manager simultaneously.
4. If the emergency involves fire, medical distress, or immediate life safety risk, Front Desk or the detecting employee calls external emergency services (911 or local equivalent) without delay.
5. Duty Manager proceeds immediately to the location or command point to assume Incident Commander role.

**Quality Checkpoints:** Code Alert activated within 60 seconds of initial notification; external emergency services contacted within 2 minutes for life-safety incidents.

**Expected Outcome:** All relevant personnel are notified and mobilized within minutes of emergency detection.

**Escalation Conditions:** Any delay in reaching the Duty Manager triggers automatic escalation to the next-ranking manager on the property's emergency contact chain.

---

### Phase 2: Situation Assessment and Response Declaration

**Purpose:** To determine the appropriate protective action — evacuation, shelter-in-place, or contained response — based on the nature of the emergency.

**Required Materials:** Property floor plans, emergency response level reference guide, radio system.

**Step-by-Step Process:**
1. Duty Manager or Incident Commander assesses the situation using available information from the reporting employee, Security, and Engineering.
2. Determine the appropriate response: full evacuation, partial/horizontal evacuation, shelter-in-place, or contained departmental response.
3. Communicate the declared response level to all department heads via the Code Alert System.
4. Assign Security and Housekeeping to floor sweep duties if evacuation is declared.
5. Assign Front Desk to prepare and maintain the in-house guest and employee count for accountability.

**Quality Checkpoints:** Response level declared within 5 minutes of confirmed emergency.

**Expected Outcome:** All staff understand and begin executing a single, coordinated response.

**Escalation Conditions:** If the situation exceeds property capability to manage safely, the Incident Commander defers full control to arriving first responders immediately.

---

### Phase 3: Evacuation Execution

**Purpose:** To move guests and employees safely and efficiently away from the hazard to designated Assembly Points.

**Required Materials:** Evacuation route maps, Assembly Point signage, headcount lists, radios.

**Step-by-Step Process:**
1. Employees on each floor direct guests toward the nearest marked exit, never using elevators during a fire evacuation.
2. Housekeeping and Security conduct floor sweeps, knocking on doors and checking common areas for remaining guests.
3. Employees assist guests with mobility limitations using designated evacuation chairs or refuge areas per accessibility protocol.
4. All evacuated guests and employees proceed to the designated Assembly Point.
5. Front Desk or a designated employee conducts a headcount at the Assembly Point, cross-referencing against the in-house guest list.
6. Any unaccounted-for guests or employees are reported immediately to the Incident Commander and first responders.

**Quality Checkpoints:** Full building evacuation initiated within 3 minutes of declaration; Assembly Point headcount underway within 10 minutes of evacuation start.

**Expected Outcome:** All guests and employees are accounted for at a safe location away from the hazard.

**Escalation Conditions:** Any unaccounted-for individual is immediately reported to first responders as a priority search location.

---

### Phase 4: First Responder Coordination

**Purpose:** To ensure a smooth, informative handoff of incident control to arriving emergency services.

**Required Materials:** Property floor plans, hazard location notes, guest/employee headcount records.

**Step-by-Step Process:**
1. Designated employee (typically Security or Duty Manager) meets first responders at the property entrance to guide them to the incident location.
2. Provide first responders with floor plans, known hazard details, and current headcount status.
3. Duty Manager remains available as the on-site point of contact but defers operational control to first responders upon their arrival.
4. Continue supporting guest communication and comfort at the Assembly Point while first responders manage the incident.

**Quality Checkpoints:** First responders met and briefed within 2 minutes of arrival on property.

**Expected Outcome:** First responders have all necessary information to manage the incident efficiently and safely.

**Escalation Conditions:** None; this phase operates under first responder authority once they arrive.

---

### Phase 5: All Clear and Recovery

**Purpose:** To safely resume normal operations only after the emergency has been fully resolved and the property has been declared safe.

**Required Materials:** All Clear declaration from first responders or Duty Manager, incident documentation forms.

**Step-by-Step Process:**
1. Duty Manager receives confirmation from first responders (if involved) that the property is safe for re-entry.
2. Duty Manager issues the "All Clear" declaration via the Code Alert System.
3. Guests and employees are guided back into the building in an organized manner.
4. Incident Commander completes the Incident Report Form documenting the timeline, actions taken, and any injuries or damages.
5. Duty Manager briefs the General Manager and relevant department heads on next steps, including guest communication, compensation, or facility repairs.

**Quality Checkpoints:** Incident Report completed within 24 hours of the "All Clear" declaration.

**Expected Outcome:** Normal operations resume safely, with a complete documented record of the incident.

**Escalation Conditions:** Any incident involving injury, significant property damage, or media attention is escalated immediately to the General Manager and corporate risk management per property policy.

## 7. Best Practices

- **Know your evacuation routes before an emergency occurs.** Familiarity under calm conditions translates directly into faster, safer action under stress.
- **Never use elevators during a fire evacuation.** Elevators can fail, become trapped, or open directly into hazardous conditions; stairwells are the only approved evacuation route during fire events.
- **Stay calm and project calm to guests.** Guest behavior during an emergency mirrors staff demeanor; visible panic among employees significantly increases guest panic and evacuation time.
- **Never delay notifying Front Desk or Security to "confirm" a situation yourself.** Immediate notification allows parallel response actions (Code Alert, external services contact) to begin without losing critical time.
- **Defer fully to first responders upon their arrival.** Property staff support logistics and information, but trained emergency personnel must direct the operational response once on-site.

## 8. Exceptions

**Guests with Disabilities:** Evacuation procedures must account for mobility, visual, and auditory impairments; designated refuge areas and evacuation chairs must be used, and staff must never assume a guest can self-evacuate without assistance.

**VIP Guests:** Receive no special evacuation priority or delay; life safety procedures apply equally to all guests regardless of status.

**Severe Weather Events:** May require shelter-in-place rather than evacuation; Duty Manager determines the appropriate action based on official weather service guidance and property location risk factors.

**Bomb Threats:** Evacuation decisions follow law enforcement guidance; do not use radios or cell phones near the suspected device location, as electronic transmissions may pose a risk in certain threat scenarios.

**Power/System Failures:** Engineering activates backup generator and emergency lighting systems immediately; evacuation is only declared if the failure creates a direct safety hazard (e.g., loss of fire suppression system).

**Active Threat Situations:** Staff follow "Run, Hide, Fight" guidance as trained, prioritizing personal and guest safety over property protection; law enforcement assumes full incident control upon arrival.

## 9. Troubleshooting

**Problem: Fire alarm activates but no visible fire or smoke is found.**
- *Possible Causes:* False alarm, sensor malfunction, steam from a guest bathroom, or an undetected fire in a concealed location.
- *Resolution:* Treat as a genuine emergency until confirmed otherwise by Security or Engineering; do not silence the alarm or delay Code Alert activation.
- *Escalation:* Duty Manager determines evacuation necessity even in the absence of visible fire.

**Problem: A guest refuses to evacuate.**
- *Possible Causes:* Guest disbelief in the threat, mobility concerns, guest belongings left in room.
- *Resolution:* Communicate the urgency clearly and calmly; do not physically force a guest, but document the refusal and inform first responders upon arrival.
- *Escalation:* Report the room number and refusal immediately to the Incident Commander and first responders.

**Problem: Headcount at the Assembly Point does not match the in-house guest list.**
- *Possible Causes:* Guest already checked out but not updated in PMS, guest was off-property at time of incident, guest missed the sweep.
- *Resolution:* Report all discrepancies immediately to first responders for priority search consideration; do not assume an unaccounted guest is simply "off-site."
- *Escalation:* Immediate and continuous until confirmed resolved by first responders.

**Problem: Radio communication fails during an active emergency.**
- *Possible Causes:* Dead batteries, signal interference, system overload.
- *Resolution:* Switch to backup communication method (runner system, mobile phones, or secondary radio channel) immediately.
- *Escalation:* Report communication failure to Engineering and Duty Manager for immediate resolution.

**Problem: Employees are unsure of their designated emergency role during an incident.**
- *Possible Causes:* Insufficient drill participation, new employee onboarding gap.
- *Resolution:* Default to reporting to your department head or the nearest Assembly Point and following visible staff direction.
- *Escalation:* Report training gaps to the Director of Security & Loss Prevention for inclusion in the next drill cycle.

## 10. Frequently Asked Questions

**1. What is the first thing I should do if I discover a fire?**
Immediately activate the nearest fire alarm pull station, then notify Front Desk or Security, and begin evacuating the immediate area.

**2. Can I use the elevator during a fire evacuation?**
No. Elevators must never be used during a fire evacuation; use the nearest stairwell.

**3. What is a Code Alert, and who uses it?**
The Code Alert System is the property's internal coded communication protocol used by Front Desk and department heads to notify staff of an emergency without alarming guests.

**4. Who has the authority to declare an evacuation?**
The Duty Manager, acting as Incident Commander, has the authority to declare an evacuation or other protective action.

**5. Where is the Assembly Point located?**
Assembly Point locations are property-specific and posted on evacuation route maps in employee and guest areas; confirm your property's designated location during onboarding and drills.

**6. What should I do if a guest with a mobility impairment needs help evacuating?**
Direct them to the nearest designated refuge area if immediate evacuation is not possible, and immediately inform Security or first responders of their exact location.

**7. What is the difference between evacuation and shelter-in-place?**
Evacuation involves moving guests and employees out of the building, while shelter-in-place directs them to remain indoors, typically used during severe weather or specific security threats.

**8. Who do I contact first if I witness a medical emergency?**
Contact Front Desk or Security immediately, who will activate the Code Alert System and contact emergency medical services.

**9. Where are AEDs located on property?**
AED locations are property-specific and marked on posted emergency equipment maps; all employees should know the nearest AED location to their work area.

**10. What do I do if I receive a bomb threat call?**
Remain calm, document as much information as possible without alarming the caller, and immediately notify Security and the Duty Manager without disconnecting the call if possible.

**11. Should I fight a small fire myself?**
Only if you are trained and the fire is in its incipient stage (small and contained); otherwise, evacuate the area and notify Security immediately.

**12. What happens after the "All Clear" is declared?**
Guests and employees are guided back into the building in an organized manner, and an Incident Report is completed documenting the event.

**13. Who is responsible for headcount accountability during an evacuation?**
Front Desk maintains and cross-references the in-house guest list against the Assembly Point headcount, in coordination with department heads.

**14. What should I do during a severe weather shelter-in-place order?**
Move guests and employees away from windows to designated interior shelter areas and remain there until the Duty Manager issues further instructions.

**15. Can guests re-enter the building before the "All Clear" is declared?**
No. Re-entry is only permitted after the Duty Manager or first responders formally declare the "All Clear."

**16. What is my role if I am not part of the Emergency Response Team?**
Follow the direction of the ERT and first responders, assist guests calmly, and proceed to the Assembly Point as instructed.

**17. How often are emergency drills conducted?**
Emergency drills are conducted quarterly for all staff and documented per property policy.

**18. What do I do if radios stop working during an emergency?**
Switch to the backup communication method (runner system or mobile phones) and notify Engineering and the Duty Manager of the failure.

**19. Who talks to the media if an emergency incident occurs?**
Only the General Manager or a designated corporate communications representative is authorized to speak with media; all other employees should direct inquiries to the General Manager.

**20. What should I document after an emergency incident?**
Complete the Incident Report Form with a full timeline, actions taken, individuals involved, and any injuries or damages, within 24 hours of the All Clear.

## 13. Training Scenarios

### Scenario 1: Fire Alarm Activation During Peak Occupancy
**Situation:** The fire alarm activates at 7:00 PM during a fully booked evening with a large event in progress in the ballroom.

**Correct Response:** All staff immediately begin evacuation procedures, directing guests to the nearest stairwell exits; Food & Beverage staff assist with headcount and calm crowd movement from the event space; Front Desk activates the Code Alert System and prepares the in-house guest list.

**Incorrect Response:** Staff waiting to confirm whether the alarm is false before beginning any evacuation action, or allowing guests to use elevators to expedite movement.

**Supervisor Notes:** Every alarm activation is treated as genuine until confirmed otherwise by Security or Engineering; delay-based responses cost critical time.

**Learning Outcome:** Immediate, uniform action during any alarm activation is essential regardless of perceived likelihood of a false alarm.

---

### Scenario 2: Guest Reports Chest Pain in the Lobby
**Situation:** A guest suddenly reports chest pain and shortness of breath while checking in at the Front Desk.

**Correct Response:** The Front Desk Agent immediately calls emergency medical services, notifies Security and the Duty Manager, and directs a colleague to retrieve the nearest AED while staying with the guest and keeping them calm.

**Incorrect Response:** Asking the guest to wait while attempting to locate a manager first, or assuming the situation is not serious enough to warrant an emergency call.

**Supervisor Notes:** Time-critical medical emergencies require immediate parallel action — calling emergency services and retrieving life-safety equipment — not sequential decision-making.

**Learning Outcome:** All employees must be empowered and trained to initiate emergency medical response without waiting for managerial approval.

---

### Scenario 3: Severe Weather Warning Issued During Guest Check-In Rush
**Situation:** A tornado warning is issued for the immediate area while the Front Desk is handling a high volume of afternoon check-ins.

**Correct Response:** Duty Manager declares shelter-in-place, and all staff calmly direct guests and colleagues to designated interior shelter areas away from windows, pausing check-in operations until the warning is lifted.

**Incorrect Response:** Continuing standard check-in operations near exterior windows without pausing for the shelter-in-place directive.

**Supervisor Notes:** Operational tasks are always secondary to life-safety directives; check-in service is paused immediately upon a shelter-in-place declaration.

**Learning Outcome:** Life-safety protective actions take precedence over any operational or service standard, without exception.
