## 1. Purpose

This Food Safety Standard Operating Procedure (SOP) establishes the mandatory practices for safe food handling, storage, preparation, and service across all Food & Beverage outlets, including restaurants, bars, banquet/event kitchens, room service, and employee dining facilities. It ensures compliance with food safety regulations, protects guest and employee health, and minimizes the risk of foodborne illness through consistent temperature control, hygiene, cross-contamination prevention, and allergen management practices.

This document also defines critical control points, HACCP-aligned monitoring practices, and incident response procedures, and serves as an authoritative reference within the AI Operations Assistant knowledge base for food safety-related employee questions.

---

## 2. Scope

This SOP applies to all employees who handle, prepare, store, or serve food and beverages on property, including:

- Culinary staff (Chefs, Cooks, Stewards)
- Restaurant and Bar servers and bartenders
- Room Service staff
- Banquet and Catering staff
- Employee Dining Room staff
- Purchasing and Receiving staff handling food deliveries

This SOP covers receiving and storage, temperature control, food preparation and cooking, holding and service, cleaning and sanitation, allergen management, and illness/incident reporting. It does not cover general Housekeeping cleaning procedures for non-food areas or Front Desk operations, which are addressed in separate departmental SOPs.

## 3. Definitions

- **Temperature Danger Zone**: The temperature range (commonly 41°F–135°F / 5°C–57°C) within which bacteria multiply rapidly in food and which must be minimized in exposure time.
- **HACCP (Hazard Analysis and Critical Control Points)**: A systematic food safety management approach identifying and controlling critical points in the food handling process where hazards can occur.
- **Critical Control Point (CCP)**: A specific step in food handling (e.g., cooking, cooling, holding) where control is essential to prevent or eliminate a food safety hazard.
- **Cross-Contamination**: The transfer of harmful bacteria or allergens from one food, surface, or utensil to another.
- **FIFO (First In, First Out)**: An inventory rotation method ensuring older stock is used before newer stock to minimize spoilage risk.
- **Allergen**: A substance capable of causing an allergic reaction; commonly monitored allergens include peanuts, tree nuts, shellfish, dairy, eggs, soy, wheat/gluten, and sesame.
- **Time-Temperature Abuse**: Any instance where food remains in the Temperature Danger Zone longer than permitted, increasing bacterial growth risk.
- **Sanitizing**: The process of reducing pathogens on a surface to a safe level, typically following cleaning, using an approved chemical or heat-based method.
- **Foodborne Illness Incident**: Any reported or suspected case of illness linked to food consumed on property, requiring immediate documentation and investigation.
- **Person in Charge (PIC)**: The designated, food-safety-certified individual responsible for oversight of food safety compliance during a given shift.

## 4. Roles & Responsibilities

### Culinary Staff (Chefs, Cooks)
- Follow proper cooking temperatures and cooking-time standards for all menu items.
- Monitor and document temperature logs for cooking, cooling, and holding.
- Prevent cross-contamination through proper equipment and surface separation.
- Report any food safety concerns immediately to the Executive Chef or Sous Chef.

### Stewarding
- Maintains proper cleaning and sanitizing of kitchen equipment, dishware, and surfaces.
- Ensures dish machines and sanitizing stations operate at required temperature/chemical concentration.
- Manages proper waste disposal and pest control housekeeping standards in back-of-house areas.

### Restaurant / Bar / Room Service Staff
- Handles food and beverage service in a manner that maintains temperature and prevents contamination.
- Communicates allergen information accurately to guests and relays allergy notifications to the kitchen.
- Reports any guest-reported illness or food safety concern immediately to a Manager.

### Purchasing / Receiving Staff
- Verifies food deliveries meet temperature and quality standards upon receipt.
- Rejects any delivery showing signs of spoilage, temperature abuse, or damaged packaging.
- Ensures proper FIFO rotation labeling upon storage.

### Executive Chef / Culinary Leadership
- Serves as or designates the Person in Charge (PIC) for food safety oversight.
- Conducts regular food safety training and certification tracking for culinary staff.
- Oversees HACCP plan implementation and Critical Control Point monitoring.
- Investigates and documents any food safety incidents or illness reports.

### Director of Food & Beverage
- Ensures departmental compliance with regulatory food safety requirements.
- Reviews health inspection results and coordinates corrective action plans.
- Approves food safety training curriculum and certification requirements.

## 5. Standard Operating Procedure

### Phase 1: Receiving and Storage

**Purpose:** To ensure all incoming food products meet safety and quality standards before entering inventory.

**Required Materials:** Calibrated thermometer, receiving log, delivery invoices.

**Step-by-Step Process:**
1. Inspect all deliveries immediately upon arrival for packaging integrity, expiration dates, and visible quality.
2. Check and record the temperature of refrigerated and frozen items using a calibrated thermometer; refrigerated items must arrive at or below 41°F (5°C), frozen items solidly frozen.
3. Reject any delivery showing signs of temperature abuse, damage, pest evidence, or expired dating.
4. Label all received items with the date of receipt and rotate stock using FIFO principles.
5. Store items in the appropriate storage area (dry storage, refrigeration, freezer) immediately, avoiding extended time at room temperature.
6. Store raw proteins below and separate from ready-to-eat items to prevent cross-contamination.

**Quality Checkpoints:** 100% of deliveries temperature-checked and logged; no delivery accepted outside safe temperature range.

**Expected Outcome:** All incoming food products are verified safe and properly stored to maintain quality and safety through use.

**Escalation Conditions:** Any rejected delivery is reported immediately to the Executive Chef and Purchasing Manager for vendor follow-up.

---

### Phase 2: Food Preparation and Cross-Contamination Prevention

**Purpose:** To prepare food safely while preventing the transfer of harmful bacteria or allergens between products.

**Required Materials:** Color-coded cutting boards, sanitized utensils, handwashing stations, allergen reference guide.

**Step-by-Step Process:**
1. Wash hands thoroughly before beginning food preparation and after any interruption (e.g., touching raw meat, phone use, trash handling).
2. Use designated color-coded cutting boards and utensils for raw proteins, produce, and ready-to-eat items.
3. Sanitize all surfaces and equipment between different food types, particularly after raw protein contact.
4. Verify allergen requirements for any flagged orders and use separate preparation equipment when preparing allergen-free items.
5. Minimize the time perishable ingredients spend outside of temperature-controlled storage during prep.

**Quality Checkpoints:** Zero cross-contamination incidents; 100% compliance with color-coded equipment usage.

**Expected Outcome:** Food is prepared safely with no cross-contact between raw, ready-to-eat, and allergen-restricted items.

**Escalation Conditions:** Any suspected cross-contamination event is reported immediately to the Executive Chef, and affected food is discarded rather than served.

---

### Phase 3: Cooking and Temperature Control

**Purpose:** To ensure all food reaches safe internal cooking temperatures to eliminate harmful pathogens.

**Required Materials:** Calibrated food thermometer, cooking temperature reference chart, temperature log.

**Step-by-Step Process:**
1. Cook food to the minimum internal temperature required for the specific protein or item type (e.g., poultry to 165°F/74°C, ground meats to 155°F/68°C, whole cuts of beef/pork/fish to 145°F/63°C, per current regulatory guidance).
2. Verify internal temperature using a calibrated thermometer inserted into the thickest part of the item, avoiding bone contact.
3. Record cooking temperatures on the Temperature Log for designated Critical Control Point items.
4. Hold cooked food at or above 135°F (57°C) if not served immediately, or begin proper cooling procedure if being stored for later use.
5. Cool cooked food that will be stored rapidly, moving from 135°F to 70°F within 2 hours, and from 70°F to 41°F within an additional 4 hours.

**Quality Checkpoints:** 100% of Critical Control Point items temperature-verified and logged before service.

**Expected Outcome:** All food served meets safe internal temperature standards, minimizing foodborne illness risk.

**Escalation Conditions:** Any item failing to reach or maintain safe temperature is not served and is reported to the Executive Chef for corrective action.

---

### Phase 4: Holding and Service

**Purpose:** To maintain food safety standards from kitchen completion through guest service.

**Required Materials:** Hot holding equipment, cold holding equipment, temperature log, service thermometers.

**Step-by-Step Process:**
1. Maintain hot-held food at or above 135°F (57°C) and cold-held food at or below 41°F (5°C) at all times.
2. Check and log holding temperatures at regular intervals (commonly every 2 hours) during active service periods.
3. Discard any food that has remained in the Temperature Danger Zone for more than 4 hours cumulative.
4. Serve allergen-flagged items using dedicated service equipment and clear communication to the guest confirming the allergen accommodation.
5. Room Service and Banquet staff maintain proper covered transport and minimize transit time to preserve temperature integrity.

**Quality Checkpoints:** Holding temperatures logged every 2 hours; zero service of food exceeding Danger Zone time limits.

**Expected Outcome:** Food maintains safe temperature and integrity from kitchen to guest, including for off-site or transported service.

**Escalation Conditions:** Any holding temperature deviation discovered during a check is corrected immediately, and affected food is discarded if the deviation exceeds safe limits.

---

### Phase 5: Cleaning, Sanitizing, and Illness Reporting

**Purpose:** To maintain a sanitary food handling environment and respond appropriately to any suspected foodborne illness.

**Required Materials:** Approved sanitizing chemicals, sanitizer test strips, illness reporting form.

**Step-by-Step Process:**
1. Clean and sanitize all food contact surfaces and equipment per the established cleaning schedule and after any raw protein contact.
2. Verify sanitizer concentration using test strips per manufacturer and regulatory guidance.
3. Any employee experiencing symptoms of illness (vomiting, diarrhea, jaundice, fever) must report to their Manager immediately and be excluded from food handling duties per property policy.
4. Any guest-reported illness potentially linked to food consumed on property is documented immediately using the Foodborne Illness Incident Form.
5. Executive Chef or PIC investigates the incident, reviewing relevant temperature logs, ingredient sourcing, and preparation records.

**Quality Checkpoints:** Sanitizer concentration verified at required intervals per shift; illness reports documented within 1 hour of notification.

**Expected Outcome:** A consistently sanitary food handling environment with rapid, documented response to any illness concern.

**Escalation Conditions:** Any suspected foodborne illness incident is escalated immediately to the Director of Food & Beverage and General Manager, with regulatory health authority notification per local requirements.

## 7. Best Practices

- **Calibrate thermometers regularly, not just when a reading seems questionable.** Consistent calibration prevents false confidence in inaccurate temperature readings, which is a leading root cause of undetected food safety failures.
- **Never rely on visual or smell inspection alone for safety decisions.** Many pathogens do not alter food's appearance, smell, or taste, making temperature and time controls the only reliable safety measure.
- **Communicate allergen information clearly and specifically, not generally.** Vague reassurances ("should be fine") create serious risk; confirm exact ingredients and preparation methods before serving allergen-flagged orders.
- **Treat every reported guest illness as a genuine food safety concern until ruled out.** Early, thorough documentation protects both guest health and the property's ability to investigate accurately.
- **Maintain FIFO discipline even under time pressure.** Skipping rotation during busy periods is a common root cause of otherwise avoidable spoilage-related incidents.

## 8. Exceptions

**Banquet and Off-Site Catering Events:** Require additional transport temperature monitoring and holding equipment verification at the event location; a designated PIC must be present on-site for the duration of food service.

**Room Service Extended Transit:** Items requiring extended transit time to guest rooms must use insulated transport equipment, and kitchen staff must account for transit time within the total Danger Zone exposure calculation.

**Guest-Requested Rare or Under-Cooked Preparations:** Where permitted by property policy and regulatory guidance, guests may request temperatures below standard minimums (e.g., rare beef); this must include a documented guest advisory regarding increased foodborne illness risk per regulatory disclosure requirements.

**Employee Dining Room Service:** Subject to the same food safety standards as guest-facing outlets; no reduced standard applies for internal staff meals.

**Buffet and Self-Service Stations:** Require more frequent temperature monitoring (commonly every hour) due to extended exposure time and multiple points of guest contact.

**Known Allergy Emergencies:** If a guest reports symptoms of an allergic reaction, staff immediately contact Security/Duty Manager and emergency medical services per the Emergency Response Manual, rather than attempting to independently manage the medical situation.

## 9. Troubleshooting

**Problem: A refrigerator or walk-in cooler is found running above safe holding temperature.**
- *Possible Causes:* Equipment malfunction, door left open, overloading preventing proper airflow.
- *Resolution:* Move contents to a functioning unit immediately if safe temperature cannot be quickly restored; discard any item that has exceeded safe holding time.
- *Escalation:* Notify Engineering for equipment repair and the Executive Chef for inventory loss assessment.

**Problem: A cook is unsure whether a cooling batch of food met the required cooling time benchmarks.**
- *Possible Causes:* Missed temperature log entry, interrupted cooling process.
- *Resolution:* If cooling time cannot be verified as compliant, discard the item rather than risk service of improperly cooled food.
- *Escalation:* Report to the Executive Chef to reinforce cooling log discipline.

**Problem: A guest reports symptoms of illness after a meal served on property.**
- *Possible Causes:* Foodborne illness, unrelated illness, allergy reaction.
- *Resolution:* Document the report immediately using the Foodborne Illness Incident Form; do not admit fault or speculate on cause with the guest.
- *Escalation:* Immediate escalation to Executive Chef, Director of Food & Beverage, and General Manager; regulatory health authority notification per local requirements.

**Problem: Sanitizer test strip reading shows concentration outside the acceptable range.**
- *Possible Causes:* Improper chemical dilution, expired sanitizer solution, dispensing equipment malfunction.
- *Resolution:* Discard and remake the sanitizing solution to correct concentration before continued use.
- *Escalation:* Persistent dispensing equipment issues reported to Stewarding management and Engineering.

**Problem: An allergen-flagged order is prepared using equipment that also handled the allergen.**
- *Possible Causes:* Miscommunication of allergy flag, insufficient equipment separation during a busy service period.
- *Resolution:* Do not serve the item; remake using fully separated equipment and clearly communicate the delay to the guest with an appropriate apology.
- *Escalation:* Report to the Executive Chef to reinforce allergen protocol compliance.

## 10. Frequently Asked Questions

**1. What is the safe internal cooking temperature for chicken?**
Poultry, including chicken, must reach a minimum internal temperature of 165°F (74°C).

**2. How long can food safely sit in the Temperature Danger Zone?**
No more than 4 hours cumulative between 41°F and 135°F before it must be discarded.

**3. What do I do if a delivery arrives with a damaged package?**
Reject the delivery if packaging integrity is compromised, and document the rejection for Purchasing follow-up with the vendor.

**4. How often should I wash my hands during food prep?**
Before beginning prep, after handling raw proteins, after touching your face or phone, after using the restroom, and any time contamination risk is introduced.

**5. What color cutting board should I use for raw chicken?**
Follow the property's color-coding system; raw poultry is typically assigned a dedicated color separate from produce and ready-to-eat items — confirm your property's specific chart.

**6. Can I serve a guest's steak rare if they request it?**
Only if permitted by property policy and accompanied by the required regulatory guest advisory regarding increased foodborne illness risk from undercooked proteins.

**7. What should I do if I feel sick during my shift?**
Report your symptoms to your Manager immediately; you will be excluded from food handling duties per property illness policy.

**8. How do I handle a guest's allergy request?**
Confirm the specific allergen, verify ingredient and preparation safety with the kitchen, and use dedicated equipment to avoid cross-contact.

**9. What temperature should hot-held food be maintained at during service?**
At or above 135°F (57°C) at all times during holding.

**10. How do I know if my sanitizer solution is at the correct concentration?**
Test it using sanitizer test strips per manufacturer guidance at the required frequency for your station.

**11. What is FIFO, and why does it matter?**
First In, First Out is the practice of using older stock before newer stock to minimize spoilage risk and maintain product quality.

**12. What do I do if I find food in the walk-in cooler with no date label?**
Do not use it; report it to your Supervisor, as undated product cannot be verified for safe shelf life compliance.

**13. Can I use the same cutting board for raw fish and vegetables if I wash it in between?**
No. Use separate, color-coded cutting boards designated for each food category rather than relying solely on washing between uses.

**14. What should I do if a guest reports feeling sick after eating at the property?**
Document the report immediately using the Foodborne Illness Incident Form and notify your Manager; do not speculate on the cause with the guest.

**15. How quickly must cooked food be cooled if it's being stored for later use?**
From 135°F to 70°F within 2 hours, then from 70°F to 41°F within an additional 4 hours.

**16. Are buffet stations held to the same temperature standards as plated service?**
Yes, with more frequent monitoring due to extended exposure time and guest self-service contact.

**17. What do I do if the walk-in cooler temperature reading looks abnormally high?**
Move perishable contents to a functioning unit if possible, notify Engineering and the Executive Chef immediately, and do not use any item that may have exceeded safe temperature.

**18. Who is the "Person in Charge" during my shift?**
The designated, food-safety-certified individual overseeing food safety compliance, typically the Executive Chef, Sous Chef, or a designated certified Manager on duty.

**19. Do employee meals need to follow the same food safety standards as guest meals?**
Yes. Employee Dining Room service is held to the same food safety standards as any guest-facing outlet.

**20. What is a Critical Control Point, and why is it logged?**
It is a specific step in food handling (e.g., cooking, cooling) where a hazard must be controlled; logging ensures verification that safety standards were met at each critical step.

## 13. Training Scenarios

### Scenario 1: Walk-In Cooler Temperature Failure Discovered Mid-Shift
**Situation:** A cook notices the walk-in cooler thermometer reading 48°F (9°C) instead of the required 41°F (5°C) or below, and unsure how long it has been elevated.

**Correct Response:** Immediately notify the Executive Chef, move perishable items to a functioning unit if possible, and discard any item that cannot be verified as having stayed within safe temperature limits for the required duration.

**Incorrect Response:** Assuming the food is still safe because it "looks fine" and continuing to use it without verification.

**Supervisor Notes:** Temperature deviations must always be treated as a food safety failure requiring verification, not visual judgment.

**Learning Outcome:** Food safety decisions must be based on documented temperature and time data, never appearance or assumption.

---

### Scenario 2: Guest Requests a Menu Item Modified for a Severe Nut Allergy
**Situation:** A guest at dinner service discloses a severe tree nut allergy and asks whether a specific dish is safe to order.

**Correct Response:** The server confirms the exact ingredients and preparation method with the kitchen before responding, and the kitchen prepares the item using dedicated, allergen-free equipment with clear communication back to the server and guest.

**Incorrect Response:** Assuring the guest the dish is "probably fine" without confirming with the kitchen.

**Supervisor Notes:** Allergy communication requires verified, specific confirmation at every step — server to kitchen and kitchen to server — never assumption-based reassurance.

**Learning Outcome:** Allergen safety depends on precise, verified communication across every point of the service chain.

---

### Scenario 3: Employee Reports Feeling Ill Before a Busy Shift
**Situation:** A line cook scheduled for a fully booked dinner service reports feeling nauseous and having had a fever earlier in the day.

**Correct Response:** The Manager immediately excludes the employee from food handling duties per illness policy, regardless of staffing pressure, and arranges appropriate coverage.

**Incorrect Response:** Allowing the employee to work because the kitchen is short-staffed and the symptoms seem mild.

**Supervisor Notes:** Illness exclusion policy is non-negotiable regardless of operational pressure; the risk of foodborne illness transmission outweighs staffing convenience.

**Learning Outcome:** Food safety illness protocols must be enforced consistently, even when doing so creates operational difficulty.
